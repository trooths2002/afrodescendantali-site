Nigeria Refinery Power, Ownership, and Energy Security

Published May 19, 2026

Refinery politics exposes one of the central questions in Black-world economics: who controls strategic infrastructure?

Nigeria's refinery debate is not just a business story. It is a sovereignty story. When a major African market depends on imported refined fuel, every subsidy, import contract, currency shock, and shipping delay becomes part of national power.

Import dependence creates political classes

Any system that rewards import channels can produce entrenched beneficiaries. A refinery threatens that arrangement because it shifts value from foreign supply chains and domestic middlemen toward industrial ownership, processing capacity, and national bargaining power.

The diaspora capital question

Diaspora audiences often discuss investment after the fact, when the deal is already shaped. The better question is earlier: what infrastructure controls the price of life, the cost of business, and the margin available for Black capital formation?
